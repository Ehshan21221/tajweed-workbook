📖 Lesson 2: Alif Zā'id — The Extra Alif (الف زائد)
LESSON 2 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Understand what Alif Zā'id is, where it appears in the Quran, why it is written but NOT pronounced, and identify it in Quranic text.

📌 Definition: Alif Zā'id (الف زائد) means "extra Alif." It is written in certain Quranic words for historical orthographic reasons but is completely silent — not pronounced at all.
📍 Where Does Alif Zā'id Appear?
TypeExample WordHow WrittenHow ReadLocation in Quran
After Waw of PluralقَالُواWith extra Alif after WawQāloo (Alif silent)Very common in every Surah
After Waw of PluralجَاءُواWith extra Alif after WawJā'oo (Alif silent)Al-Baqarah, Al-Imran etc.

💡 How to Identify: Look for a small upright Alif written above a letter (called الف خنجرية — Khanjariyya / vertical Alif). It is always silent or represents a Maddah sound. Also, any Alif after واو الجماعة (plural Waw) is extra and silent.
📌 Rule: The extra Alif was included in Uthmānic script to preserve original spelling. Modern printed Qurans mark it with a small symbol. Always follow what you HEAR from your Quranic teacher, not just what is written.

📖 Lesson 3: Harakāt — The Short Vowels
LESSON 3 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Name all three Harakāt · Know their shapes, sounds, and Arabic names · Read any letter with all three vowels correctly.

📌 Definition: Harakāt (حَرَكَات) are the short vowel symbols written above or below Arabic letters. Every consonant must carry either a Harakah or a Sukūn (lesson 11).
◌َ
Fathah (فَتْحَة)
Sound: "a" — mouth opens
بَ · تَ · جَ
Written ABOVE the letter
◌ِ
Kasrah (كَسْرَة)
Sound: "i" — lower jaw drops
بِ · تِ · جِ
Written BELOW the letter
◌ُ
Dammah (ضَمَّة)
Sound: "u" — lips round/pucker
بُ · تُ · جُ
Written ABOVE the letter
⚠️ Common Mistake: Do NOT lengthen short vowels. Fathah = short "a" (not "aa"). Kasrah = short "i" (not "ee"). Dammah = short "u" (not "oo"). Lengthening only happens with Maddah letters (Lesson 7).

📖 Lesson 5: Tanwīn — The Double Vowels (ً ٍ ٌ)
LESSON 5 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Name all three types of Tanwīn · Know their shapes & sounds · Distinguish Tanwīn from single Harakāt · Apply Tanwīn rules at pause and in continuation.

📌 Definition: Tanwīn (تَنْوِين) means "nunation." It adds the sound of Nūn (ن) after the vowel at the END of a word. It only appears at word endings and is written as a double mark.
◌ً
Tanwīn Fath (تنوين فتح)
Sound: "-an" (like "an")
كِتَابًا · عَلِيمًا
Two Fathahs above — add Alif at end
◌ٍ
Tanwīn Kasr (تنوين كسر)
Sound: "-in" (like "in")
رَجُلٍ · عَظِيمٍ
Two Kasrahs below
◌ٌ
Tanwīn Damm (تنوين ضم)
Sound: "-un" (like "un")
بَيْتٌ · نُورٌ
Two Dammahs above
📌 Pausing Rule (Waqf on Tanwīn):
• Tanwīn Fath at pause → drop the Nūn, keep the Alif: كِتَابًا → كِتَابَا
• Tanwīn Kasr at pause → drop the Nūn sound: رَجُلٍ → رَجُلْ
• Tanwīn Damm at pause → drop the Nūn sound: بَيْتٌ → بَيْتْ
💡 Remember: Tanwīn follows all the same rules as Nūn Sākin (Lesson 12 & 14) — Izhār, Ikhfā', Idghām, and Iqlāb apply to Tanwīn too!

📖 Lesson 7: The Letters of Maddah — Long Vowels
LESSON 7 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Identify the 3 Maddah letters · Know their conditions · Pronounce each for exactly 2 counts (harmonic beats) · Distinguish from short vowels.

📌 Definition: Maddah (مَدَّة) means "extension/stretching." The 3 Maddah letters (حُرُوف المد) extend a vowel sound to 2 full counts: Alif (ا), Waw (و), Ya (ي).
ا
Alif Maddah
Preceded by letter with Fathah
2 counts — "aa"
قَالَ · كَانَ · بَابَ
و
Waw Maddah
Preceded by letter with Dammah + Waw Sākin
2 counts — "oo"
يَقُولُ · نُوحٌ · كُوفَة
ي
Ya Maddah
Preceded by letter with Kasrah + Ya Sākin
2 counts — "ee"
قِيلَ · فِيهِ · دِينٍ
📌 The 3 Conditions for Maddah:
Alif: the letter before must have Fathah — example: قَ + ا = قَا
Waw: the letter before must have Dammah + Waw must be Sākin — example: قُ + وْ = قُو
Ya: the letter before must have Kasrah + Ya must be Sākin — example: قِ + يْ = قِي
⚠️ Not every Waw and Ya is a Maddah letter! Only when the conditions above are met. Otherwise it is a regular letter or Lin letter (see Lesson 9).

🌙 Noor Al-Ilm AcademyLesson 8 — Kharī (Vertical) Harakāt
📖 Lesson 8: Kharī (Vertical) Harakāt — الحركات الخارية
LESSON 8 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Recognise vertical (upright) Fathah and Kasrah symbols · Understand they indicate a Maddah sound (2 counts) despite appearing like a single Harakah · Apply correctly in Quranic words.

📌 Definition: Kharī Harakāt (also called Madda Harakāt) are special vowel symbols written upright/vertically rather than horizontally. They appear in the Uthmānic script in place of the usual Alif or Ya Maddah letter and indicate a prolonged vowel of 2 counts.
📍 Types of Vertical Harakāt
SymbolNameAppearanceSoundExample WordHow to Read
◌ٰ Alif Khanjariyya
(Vertical / Dagger Alif)		 Small upright Alif written above a letter — no Alif written in the word itself "aa" — 2 counts الرَّحْمَٰنِ · لَٰكِنَّ · هَٰذَا Stretch: Raḥ-maa-ni
◌ᷰ Kasrah Muqallaba
(Vertical Kasrah)		 Kasrah written vertically (upright) below a letter instead of normal slanted position "ee" — 2 counts بِسْمِ اللهِ · فِي Stretch: Bis-mee / fee
💡 Quick Tip: Whenever you see a tiny vertical Alif sitting on top of a letter, treat it exactly like a full Alif Maddah — stretch the sound to 2 counts (aa). Same rule for vertical Kasrah → 2-count "ee" sound.
📌 Most Common Quranic Examples:
• الرَّحْمَٰنِ → Ar-Raḥmaani (vertical Alif on م)
• اللهِ → Al-laahi (implied Maddah in the name of Allah)
• هَٰذَا → Haa-dhaa (vertical Alif on ه)
• لَٰكِنَّ → Laa-kinna (vertical Alif on ل)

📖 Lesson 9: The Letters of Lin — حُرُوف اللِّين
LESSON 9 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Define Lin letters · Know their 2 conditions · Distinguish Lin from Maddah letters · Read Lin letters with ease and softness · Apply in Quranic words.

📌 Definition: Lin (لِيْن) means "softness/ease." The Letters of Lin are Waw (و) and Ya (ي) when they appear Sākin (with Sukūn) and the letter BEFORE them carries a Fathah. They are pronounced with ease — NOT stretched like full Maddah.
📍 Lin vs Maddah Comparison
FeatureMaddah LetterLin Letter
Letters usedا · و · يو · ي only
Vowel BEFORE itMust match (Fath→ا, Damm→و, Kasr→ي)Always Fathah before both و and ي
Duration2 counts (full stretch)Soft ease — 2 counts only if followed by Hamzah/Sukūn
Sound feelFull, extendedSoft, easy, gentle
Exampleقُولُوا · فِيهِخَوْفٌ · بَيْتٌ
⚠️ Do NOT confuse: في (fee) — this is Maddah (Kasrah before ي). بَيْتٌ (bayt) — this is Lin (Fathah before ي Sākin). The vowel before determines which one!

📖 Lesson 10: Read Both Ways — Rawan (روان) & Hijjay (هِجَّاي)
LESSON 10 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Understand the difference between Rawan (flowing reading) and Hijjay (letter-by-letter spelling) · Practice the same passages both ways · Build fluency AND foundational accuracy simultaneously.

📌 Definitions:
Rawan (روان): Smooth, flowing reading — you connect the letters and read the word as a whole sound without stopping.
Hijjay (هِجَّاي): Spelling reading — you say each letter individually by name before reading the full word. Example: ب + ا + ب = باب → you say: با + با = باب
📍 How to Practise Both Methods
MethodHow to Do ItPurposeWhen Used
RawanRead the full word smoothly: بَابٌ → "baabun"Build fluency & speedRegular Quran recitation
HijjaySpell letter by letter: بَ + ابٌ → "ba" + "abun" → "baabun"Accuracy, letter recognition, correct joiningLearning new words, beginners, correcting mistakes

🌙 Noor Al-Ilm AcademyLesson 11 — Sukūn (Jazm)
📖 Lesson 11: Sukūn (Jazm) — The Resting Sign (ْ)
LESSON 11 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Define Sukūn · Recognise its symbol · Know it means NO vowel · Read Sākin letters correctly · Identify Sukūn in Quranic words.

📌 Definition: Sukūn (سُكُون) means "stillness/rest." It is the small circle (ْ) written above a letter to indicate that the letter has NO vowel — the sound stops/rests at that letter. A letter with Sukūn is called Sākin (سَاكِن).
◌ْ
Sukūn / Jazm
Small circle above a letter
بْ · تْ · كْ
No vowel — letter is still
📌 Key Rules for Sākin Letters:

1. A Sākin letter is always in the MIDDLE of a word — never the first or last letter in normal recitation.

2. You do NOT add any vowel sound to a Sākin letter — just close at that letter quickly.

3. Special Sākin letters (Nūn & Meem) have extra rules → see Lessons 12, 14, and 15.

4. At Waqf (pause), the last letter also becomes effectively Sākin — see Lesson 21.

📍 Rule 1: Izhār (إِظْهَار) — Clear Pronunciation

📖 Lesson 13: Tashdīd — The Double Consonant (ّ)
LESSON 13 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Define Tashdīd · Know its symbol · Understand it represents a doubled letter (Sākin + the same letter with a vowel) · Read letters with Tashdīd with full stress · Distinguish Ghunnah Tashdīd (ن · م) from regular Tashdīd.

📌 Definition: Tashdīd (تَشْدِيد) means "to strengthen/stress." The symbol (ّ) — a small W-shape above a letter — indicates that the letter is doubled: first a Sākin version, then the same letter with its vowel. Total duration = 2 beats.

📌 Ghunnah Mushaddadah Rule: Nūn with Tashdīd (نَّ) or Meem with Tashdīd (مَّ) → hold the nasal Ghunnah for 2 full counts, then release the vowel. This applies EVERYWHERE these appear in the Quran without exception.

📍 Rule 3: Idghām (إِدْغَام) — Merging
Meaning: Idghām = "to merge/assimilate." The Nūn sound merges INTO the next letter so only the next letter's sound remains (with Tashdīd).
Letters (6): ي · ر · م · ل · و · ن — Mnemonic: يَرْمَلُوْن (Yarmaluun)
Sub-types:
• Idghām WITH Ghunnah (4 letters): ي · ن · م · و — merge AND keep nasal Ghunnah 2 counts
• Idghām WITHOUT Ghunnah (2 letters): ل · ر — merge completely, NO nasal sound

📖 Lesson 16: Tafkhīm & Tarqīq — Heavy & Light Pronunciation
LESSON 16 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Define Tafkhīm and Tarqīq · Know the 7 always-heavy letters (Musta'liyah) · Understand the rules for Ra (ر) — heavy or light · Know the Lam of Allah rule · Apply in Quranic recitation.

📌 Tafkhīm (تَفْخِيم): "Inflation/heaviness." The tongue rises toward the upper palate, giving a thick, full-mouth sound. Mouth feels "filled."
📌 Tarqīq (تَرْقِيق): "Thinning/lightness." The tongue stays low and flat, giving a thin, forward sound. Most letters are naturally light (Tarqīq).
📍 The 7 Always-Heavy Letters (حروف الاستعلاء / Musta'liyah)
The 7 Heavy Letters — Mnemonic: خُصَّ ضَغْطٍ قِظْ
خ   ص   ض   غ   ط   ق   ظThese 7 are ALWAYS heavy — in every position and with every vowel. The mouth is always "full" when saying these.

📖 Lesson 20: Miscellaneous Rules — Special Cases in Quran

🎯 Objective

📍 Rule 1: Lam Shamsiyyah vs Lam Qamariyyah (لام الشمسية و لام القمرية)
Lam Qamariyyah (Moon Lam): The Lam of الـ is pronounced clearly when followed by one of the 14 Qamar (moon) letters. You hear a clear Lam.
Moon letters mnemonic: ابغ حجك وخف عقيمه → ا ب غ ح ج ك و خ ف ع ق ي م ه

Sun letters: All 14 letters NOT in the moon list = ت ث د ذ ر ز س ش ص ض ط ظ ل ن

📍 Rule 3: Nūn Al-Wiqāyah (نون الوقاية)
When certain particles (like بِي · لِي · مِنِّي) attach to a following word beginning with Hamzatul Wasl, a protecting Nūn is inserted to prevent the vowels clashing. This Nūn is called Nūn al-Wiqāyah (protecting Nūn) and is written small.

📖 Lesson 21: Waqf — Rules of Pausing & Stopping (أَحْكَامُ الْوَقْفِ)
LESSON 21 OF 21

🎯 Objective

Define Waqf · Know all Waqf symbols used in the Quran · Apply correct stopping technique at each symbol · Know what happens to Tanwīn, Tashdīd, and Maddah at Waqf.

📌 Definition: Waqf (وَقْف) means "to stop/pause." In Quranic recitation, Waqf refers to stopping the voice at the end of a word with a brief breath, cutting the sound while keeping the mouth ready to resume. Waqf is a major science with its own rules.
📍 Waqf Symbols in the Quran (Ḥafs Recitation)
SymbolNameMeaningRule
مWaqf Lāzim (وقف لازم)Necessary Stop — MUST stop hereStopping is obligatory. Continuing changes the meaning badly.
طWaqf Muṭlaq (وقف مطلق)Absolute StopStopping is preferred / better here.
جWaqf Jā'iz (وقف جائز)Permitted StopMay stop or continue — both are fine.
زWaqf Mujawwaz (وقف مجوز)Allowed but Continuing BetterContinuing is preferred; stopping is permitted.
صWaqf Murakhkhas (وقف مرخص)Permitted due to long āyahOnly stop here if breath is needed — long verse.
لاWaqf Māni' (وقف مانع)Prohibited Stop — do NOT stopStopping is forbidden — it badly damages the meaning.
قلىAl-Waqf Awlā (الوقف أولى)Stopping is better/preferredBetter to stop but continuation is permissible.
صلىAl-Wasl Awlā (الوصل أولى)Continuing is betterBetter to continue without stopping.
∴ ∴Waqf Mu'ānaqa / MurāqabahStop at ONE — not bothTwo marked points: stop at one of them, NOT both.
